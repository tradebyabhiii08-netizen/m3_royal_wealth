🎓 XIRR vs CAGR vs Absolute Return — What's the Difference?

Three ways to measure the same portfolio. They almost always disagree — here's why each matters.
Absolute Return RAW
{{ "%.2f"|format(result.abs_return) }}%
(Received − Invested) / Invested × 100
Total % gain over the entire holding period. Ignores how long the money was invested — a 50% return over 1 year and over 10 years look identical here.
CAGR SIMPLE
{{ "%.2f"|format(_cagr) }}%
(End / Start)^(1/Years) − 1
Annual growth rate, but assumes a single lump-sum investment. Wrong for SIPs or multiple cashflows — every rupee is treated as if invested on day one.
XIRR 🏆 TRUE
{{ "%.2f"|format(result.xirr) }}%
Σ CF_i / (1+r)^(t_i/365) = 0
The correct annualized return for irregular cashflows. Every investment earns from its own deposit date — the gold standard for SIPs, SWPs, and real portfolios.
💡 For this portfolio: {% if result.xirr > result.abs_return %} XIRR ({{ "%.2f"|format(result.xirr) }}%) is higher than Absolute ({{ "%.2f"|format(result.abs_return) }}%) because money that went in later had less time to grow — but still earned at a solid annualized rate. {% else %} XIRR ({{ "%.2f"|format(result.xirr) }}%) is lower than Absolute ({{ "%.2f"|format(result.abs_return) }}%) because the raw % is spread across {{ "%.1f"|format(result.years) }} years of holding — annualizing reveals the true pace. {% endif %} {% if _cagr and (_cagr - result.xirr)|abs > 1 %} CAGR ({{ "%.2f"|format(_cagr) }}%) differs from XIRR because CAGR wrongly assumes everything was invested upfront — a common mistake when reporting SIP returns. {% endif %}

👉 When talking to clients, always quote XIRR. It's the only figure that answers the real question: "At what annual rate is my money actually compounding?"
